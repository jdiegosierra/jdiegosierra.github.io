Diego's Manifesto

Why this manifesto

I wrote this manifesto to bring together my views on how teams should work in a company. I focus especially on engineering teams, since those are the ones I have worked with the most, but I believe most of these ideas apply to any team where several people need to collaborate.

Some of what I write here comes from things I have seen go wrong. In my opinion, they were mistakes, though I understand others may see them simply as different ways of managing a team. This is not a description of a perfect company. It is a set of principles that I believe help teams stay healthy over time.

This manifesto also has a practical purpose. I want people to understand how I think about teams, leadership, and work culture, so that culture fit can be evaluated honestly before either side commits. Most of these ideas are not new to me. I have carried them with me for years, and with more experience behind me now, I feel even more confident saying that time has only reinforced them.

1. Respect #

Respect is probably the most important point in this manifesto, and ironically, although everyone would agree that it is obvious, the reality is that in practice it breaks in many ways. You do not need to insult someone to disrespect them. And in tense situations, keeping control becomes much harder.

Mistakes I have seen

Complaints about a decision with no real arguments behind them. Negative comments that add no value. Dropping out of a debate after being given counter-arguments to your idea, as if the conversation was not worth finishing. Someone expressing that they were bothered by something another person did, and that person never apologizing.

Giving harsh feedback in a public channel instead of in private. Dismissing someone's work with a label instead of offering constructive criticism. Using expertise as a constant position of authority that turns every interaction into a correction. When a communication failure happens, blaming the other person instead of acknowledging that the information did not reach them. Publicly questioning someone's ideas on social media instead of having a conversation first.

The consequences

Each of these may seem small on its own. But when they accumulate, they erode trust and create an environment where people stop engaging honestly. People become defensive, stop sharing ideas, and start avoiding conflict instead of resolving it. The team loses the ability to have healthy disagreements.

Over time, the person on the receiving end becomes permanently defensive. They start justifying every decision before anyone asks. They stop experimenting and stop taking the kind of risks that lead to growth. What could have been a productive working relationship becomes a cycle of criticism and defense that drains both sides.

How I think it should work

Respect means engaging honestly, even when you disagree. If you disagree with a decision, explain why with real arguments. If someone responds to your idea with counter-arguments, do not go silent. Respond, reconsider, or explain your position further. Silence in the middle of a debate is also a form of disrespect.

If someone tells you that something you did bothered them, acknowledge it and apologize. It does not matter whether you think they are right. What matters is that they felt it, and ignoring that feeling damages the relationship.

In tense situations, take a step back before responding. Respect is easiest to break exactly when it matters most.

2. Freedom to speak up #

Everyone in a team should feel free to share their opinion, question a decision, or suggest a different approach, regardless of their title or seniority.

Mistakes I have seen

I have worked in places where people who tried to improve things were shut down. They were told "that is not your business", "we do this because I said so", or simply "we are not going to do that, end of discussion". No explanation, no context, no room for dialogue. Just authority used as an argument.

The consequences

When people learn that speaking up leads to being dismissed, they stop trying. The team loses ideas, loses honesty, and starts operating in silence. Decisions get worse because nobody challenges them anymore. The people with the most to offer either disengage or leave.

How I think it should work

When someone says "I think we could do this another way", they deserve more than a flat no. If the idea does not work, explain why. If they are missing context, share it. If they still believe their approach is better, ask them to write a short proposal with pros and cons and review it properly. People do not need to win every argument, but they do deserve a real answer.

3. Everyone is responsible for alignment #

It is practically impossible for everyone to agree on every decision. That is natural and expected. But there is a big difference between disagreeing on trivial decisions and disagreeing on important ones. And there is a big difference between a team that is aligned on 90% of its decisions and one that is only aligned on 50%.

Mistakes I have seen

I have worked in teams where more than half the engineers were not aligned with the decisions made by the person in charge. Leaders who say "I have more context" without actually sharing that context. Leaders who decide in isolation and expect the team to follow without question.

The consequences

Having people row in a direction they are not comfortable with creates enormous friction. It leads to tension with the decision-maker, demotivation, and eventually burnout. Teams that are pushed without understanding become dependent. They stop thinking for themselves and start asking permission for everything. People stop arguing not because they agree, but because they believe arguing is useless.

How I think it should work

The person responsible for the decisions has a duty to make sure that most of the team is aligned. There may be occasional situations where that does not happen, and in those cases we accept the risk, but it should always be exceptional. If it happens often, you will have serious problems.

The team also has a responsibility to seek alignment. A team working with many split decisions creates too much friction. As I said, this should only happen exceptionally, and when it does, we accept the possible tension as a conscious tradeoff.

Leaders should avoid deciding in isolation whenever possible. I have changed my mind more than once after listening to other people, and I see that as a strength, not a weakness. There is almost always someone you can use as a sounding board: another engineer, another manager, someone from a different team, or simply someone who helps you see the blind spots in your own thinking.

Sometimes the best thing is to open the discussion to more people. Another engineer may explain the tradeoffs more clearly, or may see value in the original idea. The goal is to make better decisions while showing the same respect to everyone, from the most junior to the most senior.

All of this comes down to communication. Overcommunicate what matters, assume other people do not have your context, and do not assume bad intent when confusion is a more likely explanation. Without that context, alignment is impossible.

Alignment does not mean everyone gets the option they wanted. It means people understand the decision well enough to support it honestly, even if they would choose something else. Good teams should not work by blindly following orders. People should understand the problem, the goal, and the reason behind the decision.

4. Every team needs safe channels #

In any team, there are conversations that need their own space. Salary concerns, frictions with a colleague, disagreements with a decision, or feedback about someone's behavior — these things do not come up naturally in a group meeting or a Slack channel. If there is no structured place for them, they either stay unsaid or come out at the wrong time.

Mistakes I have seen

Teams with no 1:1s, no retrospectives, and no anonymous way to raise concerns. Everything was expected to flow through informal conversation, but sensitive topics rarely surface informally. People accumulated frustrations until they exploded or simply left.

The consequences

When there are no safe channels, problems grow in silence. People stop raising concerns because they do not know where or how. By the time someone finally speaks, the damage is already done — someone has burned out, a relationship is broken, or a bad decision has gone too far to reverse easily.

How I think it should work

Teams need real, structured spaces for honest communication. Anonymous feedback tools so people can share honest opinions about decisions, dynamics, or leadership without fear. Regular 1:1s where people can talk openly with their leader about salary, growth, concerns, and frictions. Retrospectives to dig deeper into team ideas, review what worked, and surface what did not.

These are not nice-to-haves. They are the basic infrastructure of a team that takes communication seriously. Without them, you are hoping that important things will be said — instead of making sure they can be.

5. Blame fixes nothing #

In companies, problems are rarely the fault of one person alone. When someone immediately looks for someone guilty, that should raise alarms.

Mistakes I have seen

Pointing at one person instead of looking at the real cause. Most of the time the real issue is a weak process, missing safeguards, unclear context, poor review, bad communication, or a system that made the failure easy to reach.

The consequences

At best, blaming gives someone a place to dump their frustration. At worst, it creates a chain of damage. The underlying problem remains, so the same failure will probably happen again to someone else. The person who was blamed is left working from fear, and that fear spreads fast through the rest of the team.

People who make decisions in fear stop taking healthy risks, stop asking questions, hide uncertainty, and start optimizing for self-protection instead of for the work. That is how teams become slower, less honest, and more fragile.

How I think it should work

Teams should focus on processes and learning. Postmortems matter. Looking calmly at what happened matters. Failures are natural, and expecting a system with no failures is naive. The real goal is to reduce how often failures happen, stop the same failures from repeating, and reduce the impact when they do happen.

6. You need real signals before people burn out #

Saying that employees should be monitored sounds wrong, but that is not what I mean. I do not mean spying on people. I mean having real ways to know how they are doing before they burn out. The goal is to help early, protect motivation, and keep the team healthy.

Mistakes I have seen

I have worked in startups where there were no 1:1s, no career plan, no performance reviews, and no real salary growth. Some leaders were doing a poor job. Some teammates were already burned out, which made the environment even worse. The most striking part was that HR did not know what was happening, and because of that the CEO did not know either.

Feedback saved for formal reviews is another common failure. If someone hears an important criticism or recognition for the first time in a performance review, the system has already failed.

The consequences

The company survived because the product was good and because some of us decided to act before everything broke. That is not a healthy system. A healthy company should detect these problems earlier, not when people are already at their limit.

If nobody talks to people about their satisfaction, growth, or future until they are already thinking about leaving, the company is already late. When people leave, the company loses knowledge, momentum, and a lot of time and money replacing them. People are not interchangeable parts, and turnover is much more expensive than many leaders want to admit.

How I think it should work

I believe in regular 1:1s, clear career paths, performance reviews that actually help, and spaces where people can speak honestly. A performance review is useful only if it reduces uncertainty, aligns expectations, and has real consequences for growth or salary. If it changes nothing, it is just process for the sake of process. Tools like Nailted can help, but the tool is not the point. The point is to make problems visible while there is still time to fix them.

People do not stay because of perks. They stay where they can learn, trust the team, work at a sustainable pace, and see what comes next. Motivation is not built with speeches. It grows from autonomy, learning, purpose, recognition, and real ownership.

People should have safe ways to speak, sometimes anonymously. They should be able to explain why they believe they deserve a raise. They should be able to talk about problems without feeling they are creating risk for themselves. Good leaders do not try to control employees. They try to support them and help them grow.

7. Reward people before they have to ask #

Recognition matters, but a pat on the back only goes so far. Telling someone "good job" is the first step, not the last.

Mistakes I have seen

Waiting for people to come and ask for a raise is already being late. By that point, they have probably been thinking about it for months, maybe even looking at other options. The same applies to overtime: waiting until the hours have been worked and people start complaining to figure out the compensation.

Some leaders try to save five or ten thousand by keeping salaries just low enough that the employee hesitates to leave. I have even seen leaders renegotiate a raise downward by two or three thousand to stay within budget. That kind of move destroys trust faster than almost anything else.

The consequences

When recognition comes too late, people have already made their decision. They have been feeling undervalued for months, and by the time the company reacts, loyalty is already gone. People start looking elsewhere, and the best ones leave first because they have the most options.

How I think it should work

A leader who truly pays attention can go further: "I see what you are doing these days, and when this is over I am going to fight for a raise because you deserve it." That kind of sentence, said before the employee has to ask, shows something much more powerful than gratitude. It shows that the leader is paying attention and is willing to act. The key to all of this is the same: anticipate.

If a critical delivery is coming and extra hours will be needed, say it in advance. Tell the team: "We may need some overtime for this, but we have already spoken with HR and this is how we are going to compensate it."

In most companies there are two kinds of profiles. Some people prefer to do their job well, keep things stable, and have a balanced life. That is completely legitimate. Others are more proactive, more invested, and tend to go beyond what is expected. Both profiles are valid, but the second group often creates more value over time, and they deserve to be recognized for it. Try to detect those profiles early and reward them, not just with money, but with clear signals that their effort is seen and valued.

If the company is in a healthy financial position, your employees' salary should be above market, not below or just at it. They already have something no external candidate has: deep knowledge of the codebase, the product, the team, and how things work inside the company. That knowledge has real value, and salary should reflect it.

When someone earns noticeably more than what they see in the market, they stop looking. They feel proud to work where they are, and they start recommending the company to others. That is the kind of loyalty you cannot buy with perks or speeches.

Instead of guessing, ask your employees directly: what salary would make you feel comfortable? Then work on it together. Share market data with them. Ask them for data too: offers they have seen, or even selection processes they have participated in while working at the company. Let them share that without fear. HR often has salary bands already. Share that information with your employees instead of keeping it behind closed doors.

8. Salary transparency is healthier than silence #

Salary is one of the areas where companies are often less honest than they should be.

Mistakes I have seen

Many companies do not want employees to talk openly about salary. I understand why they think it can create tension, but I often think they are really trying to avoid explaining why one person earns more than another. The same applies to new hires. If someone joins a team with a much higher salary, there should be a clear reason. If not, the difference will feel unfair.

The consequences

Telling people not to talk about salary does not solve anything. Sooner or later people talk anyway. When that happens in private, people find differences without the context behind them. Then confusion and frustration grow. Leaders often do not see the damage early enough, and the team fills the gap with assumptions. That can easily turn into distrust, resentment, and burnout.

How I think it should work

I am not saying every employee's exact salary must be public. But people should have a clear idea of the range for each role. That is a reasonable middle ground. I have not worked at a company with fully public salaries yet, so maybe there are problems I have not seen. Still, for now I believe more transparency is healthier than forced silence.

Existing employees already have valuable knowledge of the codebase, the product, and the company's way of working. That value matters too. Salary decisions should reflect it, and leaders should explain them clearly enough to keep the team aligned.

Clear salary bands and clear promotion criteria make these conversations fairer. Salary, role, and performance should not live in separate systems. When those things are disconnected, people are left guessing, and that is where distrust starts.

9. Flexibility needs trust, not control #

I have been lucky enough not to work in companies with excessive control over schedules. Most companies get this one right because extreme rigidity affects everyone and the pushback is immediate. But there is still room to improve.

Mistakes I have seen

Respecting core hours but not respecting the hours outside of them. Expecting everyone to follow the same schedule even when it does not affect the work or the team's ability to coordinate.

The consequences

People forced into a rigid schedule that does not match their natural rhythm produce less and enjoy less. They lose a sense of autonomy, which is one of the strongest drivers of motivation.

How I think it should work

It makes sense to have core hours, at least four hours where everyone overlaps and meetings can happen. But if someone prefers to start early and leave early, or work late at night, that should be fine as long as the work gets done and the team can coordinate.

I am much more productive at night. There are no distractions, no pending calls to make before something closes, no interruptions. Everything outside work is already settled for the day, and I can focus completely. Other people feel the same way about early mornings or mid-afternoon. The specific hours do not matter. What matters is the output and the trust.

Be responsible with your own time, and trust others to be responsible with theirs. Control does not create better work. Trust does.

10. Learning should be part of the job #

Outstanding engineers usually share something beyond raw output. They may have side projects. They like to stay up to date. They enjoy learning and exploring new ideas. That curiosity is part of their value.

Engineering is a field in constant evolution, and the rise of AI has accelerated that pace dramatically. Technologies that were standard a year ago may already be outdated. In this context, having dedicated time to learn and explore is not a luxury — it is a necessity.

Mistakes I have seen

I have worked in companies where the only thing that mattered was producing. No time for learning, no budget for growth, no space to experiment. Everything was urgent all the time.

The consequences

When everything is urgent all the time, people stop improving. They lower their standards, focus only on surviving the week, and lose the space needed to learn, experiment, and do thoughtful work. That may create output for a while, but it does not create strong engineers or strong teams in the long term.

How I think it should work

Good companies should support that mindset instead of squeezing it out of people. That means budget for certifications, time for spikes, space to try new tools, workshops, and real knowledge sharing inside the team.

A strong engineering culture also appears in daily habits. Tests are part of the work, technical debt is made visible, code review is a conversation, and knowledge is shared instead of hoarded.

Beyond individual growth, giving people space to investigate and experiment opens new paths for improvement. Someone who has time to explore a new tool or approach may find a better way to solve an existing problem. That kind of exploration ends up improving their work and, ultimately, the quality of the product the company delivers.

Learning should not depend only on what people do at night or on weekends. A healthy company makes room for it as part of the job.

11. In the age of AI, soft skills matter more #

If AI makes raw technical execution easier, then soft skills matter even more than before. Knowing how to explain an idea, communicate with clarity, be assertive, handle pressure, adapt to context switching, keep learning, and teach others is now one of the most valuable things an engineer can bring to a team.

Mistakes I have seen

Recently, I have met highly technical professionals with attitudes that were clearly toxic. Some are easy to spot: they insult, threaten, point at people instead of problems, or look for someone to blame when something goes wrong.

Other profiles are more subtle and can be easier to excuse, but they still do damage through repetition. Someone may respond to a mistake with comments like "this has happened many times", give negative feedback in a public channel instead of in private, or make comparisons like "I have never seen anyone do something like this". On the surface, those may look like small remarks. In practice, they create shame, defensiveness, and silence. The pattern matters.

The consequences

A toxic person does much deeper damage than someone with weak technical judgment. They can drag down the whole team, push good people away, and make the people with the best soft skills leave first. That kind of damage is much more expensive to repair.

How I think it should work

The good news is that soft skills can be worked on and improved. In my experience, a junior with strong soft skills can create value surprisingly fast in the age of AI. That person will use AI to learn faster, ask better questions, and contribute earlier than expected because they are proactive and open to feedback.

If someone has weak technical judgment, they may still make wrong decisions. But if they are humble and healthy to work with, they will often notice, learn, and self-correct. Value those qualities at least as much as raw technical ability.

12. The way you hire is part of your culture #

Hiring should start from empathy. Treat people the way you would want to be treated when you are trying to find your place. A good interview is not an interrogation. It is a structured conversation where both sides try to understand whether the match is real.

Mistakes I have seen

Random questions, improvised interviews, and technical exercises that say little about the real job. Unreasonable response times, no space for the candidate's questions, and no feedback at the end. Hiring teams that do not understand the market, the technical context, or the business, making shallow decisions as a result.

The consequences

If people leave your process feeling smaller, confused, or disrespected, then the company has already told them something important about its culture. Bad hiring processes also lead to bad hires, missed good candidates, and a reputation that makes future hiring harder.

How I think it should work

The other day I ran an interview with another engineer from the team. When we finished, he told me he wished more interviewers worked that way because the candidate felt comfortable and everything had been explained with complete transparency. That made me happy, because that is exactly the point.

Every stage should have a clear purpose. Respect people's time, explain what you want to evaluate, keep response times reasonable, leave room for their questions, and always give feedback.

Good hiring also means designing against bias. Use structured interviews, shared criteria, and written evaluations. Do not confuse similarity with quality, first impressions with evidence, or confidence with competence.

The people who design and run the process also need training. Recruiters and interviewers should keep learning too.

13. Look inside the team before hiring outside #

Before hiring someone above the current team, companies should first look at internal promotion. In many cases that is the healthier place to start.

Mistakes I have seen

Always looking outside first when a new role appears. Hiring someone above the current employees without explaining why. Adding several people to the same team at once without planning.

The consequences

If you always look outside first, people inside the company learn a simple lesson: their growth is not the priority. Hiring above your current team can create frustration, especially if the team does not understand why that person is coming in above them. Adding too many people at once can break the team's rhythm, slow delivery, and hurt motivation.

How I think it should work

If the team is already aligned that a new role is needed, for example an architect, the situation is much easier to handle. If people understand the need, the scope, and the reason why this role is not being filled internally, the environment stays much healthier. Another good step is to open the role internally first and see whether someone wants to try.

The important thing is not to pretend current employees do not exist when a new need appears. Consider them first. Give them context. Let them grow when possible. Even when the final decision is to hire from outside, the process should show clearly that the team was respected.
